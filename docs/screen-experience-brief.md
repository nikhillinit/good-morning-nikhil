# Screen Experience Brief

The finished artifact should feel like a polished, affectionate TV parody: dark, playful, fast-moving, and easy to answer. The current output already has the right stage-show framing; the ideal version keeps that energy while making every prompt unmistakable once the UI appears.

- **Intro** — Sell the premise in one breath: this is a funny, TV-themed episode about Nikhil, not a form to wrestle with. The user should feel invited in, not trapped, and the start moment should feel intentional rather than abrupt.
- **Relationship** — Establish who the respondent is to Nikhil with warmth and zero friction. The screen should make the relationship choice feel like context-setting, not filtering, and the anonymity option should feel safe, clear, and nonjudgmental.
- **Feud** — Turn first impressions into quick, instinctive reactions. This screen should feel sharp and playful, but the prompt must stay visible enough that the user knows exactly what they are answering even if they skipped audio.
- **Sponsor** — Land the joke of a fake sponsorship without confusing the respondent. The emotional job is to feel absurd, confident, and slightly self-aware, while still making the task clear and easy to complete in one line.
- **Bachelor** — Make the respondent sort Nikhil’s strongest qualities with affectionate seriousness. The tone should feel like a rose ceremony, but the labels still need to read clearly enough that non-insiders do not feel shut out.
- **Shark** — Create a decisive yes/no moment that feels bold and high-energy. The user should feel like they are making a quick verdict on Nikhil’s value, but the interaction must still feel controlled, deliberate, and fair.
- **Survivor** — Slow the pace and make room for honesty. This should feel like a confessional: more intimate, more reflective, and more human than the joke-driven screens around it.
- **Maury** — Build the biggest reveal beat in the flow. The screen should feel dramatic, slightly chaotic, and emotionally direct, but the user should still understand that the goal is a thoughtful contrast, not a gotcha.
- **Producer notes** — Shift from parody into useful feedback. This screen should feel like a final off-camera note: grounded, specific, and generous, with the emotional target being helpful closure rather than performance.
- **Review / submit** — Reassure the respondent that their answers are preserved and worth checking. The review state should feel calm and trustworthy, with full answers readable at a glance and the submit action feeling like a confident finish.
- **Final reveal** — Deliver the payoff cleanly. The last moment should feel earned, celebratory, and warm, so the respondent leaves with a sense of completion and a small burst of delight.
